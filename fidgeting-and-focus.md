Watch someone with ADHD during a long meeting. They're clicking a pen, bouncing a leg, doodling in the margins, spinning a ring on their finger. To an observer, it looks like distraction. To the person doing it, it's often the opposite — it's what makes concentration possible.

This isn't a quirk or a bad habit. It's a neurological strategy with solid science behind it.

## Optimal stimulation theory

In the 1980s, Sydney Zentall developed optimal stimulation theory to explain why some people need more sensory input to function cognitively. The core idea: every brain has an optimal level of arousal for effective thinking. Too little stimulation, and cognition suffers — attention drifts, focus dissolves. Too much, and cognition also suffers — overwhelm, anxiety, shutdown.

For neurotypical brains, the everyday environment usually provides enough baseline stimulation to reach that optimal zone. But for people with ADHD, the baseline is lower. The brain's dopaminergic system — which regulates arousal, motivation, and attention — operates differently. The result is chronic understimulation in ordinary environments.

Fidgeting is the brain's attempt to fix this. By generating its own sensory input — movement, touch, rhythm — the nervous system raises arousal to the level where attention and cognitive function work properly. The pen clicking isn't a sign that the person isn't paying attention. It's a sign that they've found a way to pay attention.

## Stimming

Stimming — self-stimulatory behaviour — is a term used primarily in the context of autism, though it applies broadly across neurodivergent experience. Stimming includes repetitive movements (hand flapping, rocking, finger tapping), repetitive sounds (humming, clicking), or repetitive tactile input (rubbing textures, squeezing objects).

For autistic people, stimming serves multiple functions: emotional regulation, sensory processing, self-soothing, and — relevantly here — creating the conditions for cognitive function. Like fidgeting in ADHD, stimming isn't a problem to be solved. It's the nervous system self-regulating to reach a state where thinking, processing, and engaging become possible.

The parallel with neurotypical behaviour is closer than many people realise. Pen clicking. Foot tapping. Hair twirling. Doodling in meetings. Pacing while on the phone. These are all forms of self-generated sensory input that help regulate arousal and support cognitive function. The difference is one of degree and social visibility, not of kind.

## The evidence for background tasks

Research supports the broader claim that background sensory-motor activity can enhance cognitive performance. Baird et al. (2012) found that engaging in an [undemanding task during an incubation period](/problem-solving/) improved creative problem-solving more than rest, demanding tasks, or no break. The undemanding task provided exactly the kind of light background engagement that occupies the body while freeing the mind.

People with ADHD frequently report that they think more clearly while playing games they're well-practised at — games that demand a portion of attention without requiring deep focus. The game occupies just enough of the task-positive network to stabilise attention, while leaving the rest of the brain free to process, plan, and make connections.

This is the principle behind [Clear](/clear/) — a tool that creates a calibrated hand-eye coordination demand. Soft blobs of light appear on screen; the user taps, flicks, or tilts to clear them. A demand dial lets the user adjust the level of engagement until they find their personal sweet spot: enough to quiet the noise, not so much that it becomes the focus.

## Not distraction — regulation

The common misunderstanding is that fidgeting and stimming are forms of distraction. The opposite is true. For brains that need additional sensory input to reach optimal arousal, these behaviours *enable* focus. Removing them — telling someone to sit still, stop clicking, pay attention — often makes concentration harder, not easier.

The implication for anyone, neurodivergent or not, is that the conditions for clear thinking are individual. Some people think best in silence. Others need background noise, music, or physical activity. The [default mode network](/default-mode-network/) and task-positive network interact differently in different brains, and the optimal balance of stimulation varies.

Underthinking isn't one-size-fits-all. For some people, a quiet [walk](/walking-and-thinking/) is the ideal state for creative incubation. For others, it's the gentle, repetitive engagement of a background task — hands busy, mind free. [Clear](/clear/) was built for the second group: people who think better when their hands have something to do.
