In 1908, the French mathematician Henri Poincaré described a breakthrough that would become one of the most famous examples of creative incubation in scientific history. He had been working intensely on a class of mathematical functions, making no progress. Frustrated, he gave up and went on a geology field trip — nothing to do with mathematics.

While boarding a bus in Coutances, the solution arrived. Completely unbidden, Poincaré suddenly saw the connection between Fuchsian functions and non-Euclidean geometry. He hadn't been thinking about the problem at all. Or rather, he hadn't been *consciously* thinking about it.

"The changes of travel made me forget my mathematical work," he wrote. "Having reached Coutances, we entered an omnibus. At the moment when I put my foot on the step the idea came to me, without anything in my former thoughts seeming to have paved the way for it."

This pattern — intense effort, followed by disengagement, followed by spontaneous insight — appears again and again in the history of ideas.

## The incubation effect

Cognitive science has a name for this: the incubation effect. It describes the finding that people often solve problems more effectively after a break than through continuous effort. The effect was first formally studied by Graham Wallas in 1926, who proposed four stages of creative problem-solving: preparation, incubation, illumination, and verification.

In 2012, Benjamin Baird and colleagues at UC Santa Barbara provided rigorous experimental evidence. They gave participants creative problems (generating unusual uses for everyday objects), then assigned them to different break conditions: a demanding task, an undemanding task, rest, or no break. The group that engaged in an *undemanding task* — one that encouraged mind-wandering — showed substantially greater improvement on problems they'd already encountered. Neither rest, demanding tasks, nor continuous work produced the same effect.

The critical finding: it wasn't just stopping that helped. It was stopping and doing something that let the mind wander. The [default mode network](/default-mode-network/) needs a light occupation to do its best work — the same principle behind [shower thoughts](/shower-thoughts/) and [walking insights](/walking-and-thinking/).

## Why it works

When you focus intensely on a problem, you activate the task-positive network. This network is excellent at systematic, logical analysis. But it can also create what psychologists call "functional fixedness" — you get locked into a particular way of seeing the problem, unable to consider alternatives.

When you step away, the [default mode network activates](/default-mode-network/). Unlike the task-positive network, the DMN draws on your full range of stored knowledge and experience, making connections across distant domains. It doesn't follow the logical paths your conscious mind has already exhausted. It explores the space around the problem, testing combinations that focused thinking would never consider.

This is why the solution often arrives from an unexpected direction — it literally comes from a part of your cognitive architecture that the focused mind wasn't accessing.

## Poirot's little grey cells

Agatha Christie captured something real about this process in Hercule Poirot. Her detective is famous for gathering information, then sitting quietly and letting his "little grey cells" work. He doesn't solve cases through relentless investigation. He investigates, then he *stops* investigating and waits for the pieces to arrange themselves.

"It is the brain, the little grey cells on which one must rely," Poirot says. "One must seek the truth within — not without." The method is: load the problem thoroughly (focused mode), then withdraw into stillness (diffuse mode), and trust that the unconscious mind will present its findings.

It's fiction, but the cognitive science bears it out. The unconscious processing that happens during incubation isn't mystical — it's the DMN doing precisely what it evolved to do.

## Rubber duck debugging

Software developers have independently discovered a version of this. "Rubber duck debugging" is the practice of explaining your code, line by line, to a rubber duck (or any inanimate object). Surprisingly often, the act of articulating the problem reveals the solution before you finish explaining it.

This works for two reasons. First, explaining forces you to slow down and examine assumptions you've been skating past — it's a form of focused preparation. Second, the slight absurdity of talking to a duck shifts your mental state just enough to loosen the fixed perspective that was blocking you. You're loading the problem clearly, then creating a tiny gap for insight to arrive.

The broader principle is the same: the solution often already exists in your mind. The obstacle is the framework you've been using to look for it. Incubation — whether through a walk, a shower, or a conversation with a rubber duck — lets your brain try different frameworks.

## The load-then-release pattern

Incubation doesn't work without preparation. You can't step away from a problem you haven't engaged with. The pattern has two essential phases:

**Load.** Work on the problem with genuine effort. Read the material. Try approaches. Get stuck. The stuckness is important — it means you've loaded the problem into your brain with enough detail and emotional charge for the DMN to work with.

**Release.** Step away to something [moderately engaging](/shower-thoughts/). Walk. Shower. Wash dishes. Use [Clear](/clear/) to occupy your hands. The key is real disengagement — not switching to email, not scrolling your phone, not worrying about the problem. Let it go completely.

Thomas Edison and Salvador Dalí both practised a deliberate version of this. They would nap sitting up, holding objects in their hands. As they drifted into the hypnagogic state — the threshold between waking and sleep, when the DMN is particularly active — the objects would drop, waking them. They'd immediately capture whatever thoughts or images had surfaced in that liminal zone.

You don't need to go that far. But the principle is sound: the transition between engagement and disengagement is where insights live. The practice of underthinking is the practice of making that transition deliberately and frequently.
