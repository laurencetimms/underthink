In 2014, Marily Oppezzo and Daniel Schwartz at Stanford University published a study that confirmed what thinkers have known for millennia: walking makes you more creative. Across four experiments, they found that walking increased creative output by an average of 60% compared to sitting.

The effect was robust. It worked indoors on a treadmill facing a blank wall. It worked outdoors. It persisted after the walk ended — people who had recently walked continued to produce more creative ideas when they sat back down. And it was specific to divergent thinking (generating many possible ideas) rather than convergent thinking (narrowing to a single answer).

The finding was striking, but it shouldn't have been surprising. Humans have been walking to think for as long as we've been thinking at all.

## The philosophical tradition

Aristotle taught while walking. His school at the Lyceum in Athens became known as the Peripatetic school — from *peripatein*, to walk about. The Stoics took their name from the Stoa Poikile, a covered walkway in the Athenian agora where Zeno of Citium taught. Epictetus taught while walking. Marcus Aurelius likely composed parts of his *Meditations* during military marches.

Nietzsche wrote that "only thoughts reached by walking have value." Kierkegaard walked for hours through Copenhagen each day, claiming his best ideas came to him in motion. Darwin built a gravel path — his "Sandwalk" — at Down House specifically for thinking walks, circling it daily for decades. Beethoven took long walks between composing sessions, often returning with solutions to musical problems he'd been stuck on.

These weren't eccentrics. They were practitioners of a cognitive technique that neuroscience has since explained.

## Why it works

Walking creates a specific combination of conditions that support creative thinking.

**Bilateral rhythmic movement.** Walking is repetitive and symmetrical — left, right, left, right. This kind of rhythmic bilateral movement appears to promote communication between brain hemispheres and activates the [default mode network](/default-mode-network/), the brain system associated with creative incubation, memory consolidation, and making unexpected connections.

**Low cognitive demand.** For most people, walking on familiar ground requires almost no conscious thought. Your body handles the mechanics automatically, freeing your brain's executive resources for internal processing. This is the same principle behind [shower thoughts](/shower-thoughts/) — a well-practised activity that occupies the body while liberating the mind.

**Moderate physical arousal.** Walking increases heart rate slightly, improving blood flow to the brain. It triggers a mild dopamine response. It shifts the nervous system toward a state that neuroscientist Alice Flaherty has linked to increased creative drive.

**Reduced external input.** When walking — especially outdoors in a natural setting — you're removed from the screens, notifications, and demands that keep the task-positive network perpetually engaged. Attention Restoration Theory, developed by Rachel and Stephen Kaplan, suggests that natural environments provide "soft fascination" — enough gentle sensory interest to hold light attention without demanding focus.

Oppezzo and Schwartz's treadmill finding is important here. The creative boost came from the physical act of walking, not from the scenery. This suggests the motor activity itself is doing something to the brain, not just the change of environment. Though natural surroundings may add additional benefits through attention restoration.

## The critical detail

Walking helps you think, but not all thinking. The Stanford study found that walking specifically boosted *divergent* thinking — generating ideas, exploring possibilities, making unusual connections. It did not improve *convergent* thinking — the analytical narrowing required to evaluate and select ideas.

This makes walking a tool for one phase of the [creative cycle](/creative-cycles/), not the whole thing. You walk to generate. You sit to evaluate. The oscillation between these modes — between [diffuse and focused thinking](/diffuse-thinking/) — is where creative work lives.

Jonathan Schooler at UC Santa Barbara notes that even wheelchair studies show the effect comes from the physical experience of walking, not from passively watching a landscape move past. Something about the act of walking — the motor rhythm, the bilateral movement, the mild exertion — unlocks a mode of thinking that sitting cannot.

## Walking in a modern context

The challenge for modern life is that we've filled every gap with stimulation. Phones come out the moment we're not actively doing something. Podcasts and music accompany every walk. The space where mind-wandering would naturally happen — the space where the DMN does its work — gets colonised by content.

Walking for thinking means walking without input. No podcast. No music (or only ambient, non-verbal sound). No checking your phone. Just movement, environment, and whatever your mind chooses to do with the space.

The [Walk](/walk/) app was designed specifically for this. It provides gentle, generative audio that supports the contemplative state without demanding attention — background sound that fades into the environment rather than occupying the foreground. No metrics. No step counts. No goals. Just support for the kind of walking that helps you think.
